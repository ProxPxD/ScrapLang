Etymology

[edit]
\n
    \n

    Uncertain.[1] Displaced niewiasta (now considered poetic) and \u017cona (meaning narrowed down to \u201cwife\u201d). Popularized by the Polish Romantic writers in the 19th century. First attested in 1545.[2]\n

    \n
    • Possibly from Old Polish kob (\u201cpigpen\u201d) or koba (\u201cmare\u201d) (the ending -ieta is found in some contemporaneous female names (e.g. Markieta, El\u017cbieta, Greta)); however, this theory has been rejected by most etymologists.[3] Compare kobia\u0142ka.
      • \n
    • Bory\u015b asserts the term is a Polish innovation with an unclear root kob- and potentially the original suffix being extended with -ita (compare Middle Polish kobita and najmita), but he rejects the existence of the suffix -ita.[4]
      • \n
    • Ba\u0144kowski asserts that the term is not native and suggests Germanic origin, possibly a compound of Middle High German kobe (\u201csty, shed\u201d) (compare modern German Koben) and *geit (\u201cgoat\u201d) (compare modern Dutch geit), originally meaning \u201ca pen for goats\u201d but also, derogatorily, \u201can enclosure for service maidens\u201d; however, he also questions why the form is -bie- and not -be-.[5]
      • \n
    • S\u0142awski also rejects native origin but is ultimately unable to draw connections to other languages.[6]
      • \n
    • D\u0142ugosz-Kurczabowa thinks the word may be of native origin, specifically a compound of Proto-Slavic *kob\u044c (\u201cbird-flight divination\u201d) and *v\u011bta (\u201cfairy\u201d), but she also does not reject the possibility of foreign origin, suggesting, e.g., Old High German gambetta (\u201cconcubine\u201d) or even Finnish kave (\u201ca creature related to the moon, sky, or air\u201d) and Estonian kabu (\u201ccastrated animal\u201d) (compare also Proto-Samic *k\u0101p\u00ebk), as the source.[7]
    \n

    Usage notes

    [edit]
    \n
    • This word was considered derogatory in the 16th century but since the 17th century, it has been the standard, neutral term for woman.
      • \n
    • In the Central Greater Poland dialect, this word has meant only wife.

    Trivia

    [edit]
    \n

    According to S\u0142ownik frekwencyjny polszczyzny wsp\u00f3\u0142czesnej (1990), kobieta is one of the most used words in Polish, appearing 27 times in scientific texts, 47 times in news, 16 times in essays, 82 times in fiction, and 78 times in plays, each out of a corpus of 100,000 words, totaling 250 times, making it the 207th most common word in a corpus of 500,000 words.[8]\n
