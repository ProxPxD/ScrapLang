    Noun

    [edit]
    \n\n

    water (countable and uncountable, plural waters)\n

    \n
    2. (uncountable) An inorganic compound (of molecular formula H2O) found at room temperature and pressure as a clear liquid; it is present naturally as rain, and found in rivers, lakes and seas; its solid form is ice and its gaseous form is steam.\n
      By the action of electricity, the water was resolved into its two parts, oxygen and hydrogen.
      \n
      • 1805 December, Julius Griffiths, \u201cA Journey across the Desert\u201d, in The Monthly Mirror, page 362:
        It is wholly out of the power of language to convey any idea of the blissful enjoyment of obtaining water, after an almost total want of it, during eight and forty hours, in the scorching regions of an Arabian desert, in the month of July.
        • \n
      • 2013 September-October, Katie L. Burke, \u201cIn the News\u201d, in American Scientist:
        Oxygen levels on Earth skyrocketed 2.4 billion years ago, when cyanobacteria evolved photosynthesis: the ability to convert water and carbon dioxide into carbohydrates and waste oxygen using solar energy.
      \n
      1. (uncountable, in particular) The liquid form of this substance: liquid H2O.\n
        May I have a glass of water?
        \n
        Your plants need more water.
        \n
        • 1835, Sir John Ross, Sir James Clark Ross, Narrative of a Second Voyage in Search of a North-west Passage …, Volume 1, pages 284\u20135:
          Towards the following morning, the thermometer fell to 5\u00b0; and at daylight, there was not an atom of water to be seen in any direction.
          • \n
        • 2002, Arthur T. Hubbard, Encyclopedia of Surface and Colloid Science, \u2192ISBN, page 4895:
          A water drop placed on the surface of ice can either spread or form a lens depending on the properties of the three phases involved in wetting, i.e., on the properties of the ice, water, and gas phases.
          • \n
        • 2013 May 11, \u201cThe climate of Tibet: Pole-land\u201d, in The Economist, volume 407, number 8835, page 80:
          Of all the transitions brought about on the Earth\u2019s surface by temperature change, the melting of ice into water is the starkest. It is binary. And for the land beneath, the air above and the life around, it changes everything.
          • \n
        • For more quotations using this term, see Citations:water.
        2. \n
      2. (countable) A serving of liquid water.\n
        • 2006, Lori Foster, Erin McCarthy, Amy Garvey, Bad Boys of Summer, \u2192ISBN, page 91:
          Joe bustled back and offered her a glass of wine but she shook her head. \u201cJust a water, please.\u201d

    3. (alchemy, philosophy) The aforementioned liquid, considered one of the Classical elements or basic elements of alchemy.\n
      He showed me the river of living water, sparkling like crystal, flowing from the throne of God.

      The rough waters of change will bring about the calm after the storm.

    9. (colloquial, figuratively) A person's intuition.\n
      Synonym: bones
      \n
      I know he'll succeed. I feel it in my waters.

    11. (colloquial, figuratively) Something which dilutes, or has the effect of watering down.\n
      Thanks for the great video. No water, 100% substance!

    12. A particular quality or appearance suggestive of water:\n
      1. The limpidity and lustre of a precious stone, especially a diamond.\n
        a diamond of the first water is perfectly pure and transparent

        2. \n
      2. A wavy, lustrous pattern or decoration such as is imparted to linen, silk, metals, etc.

    Verb

    [edit]
    \n

    water (third-person singular simple present waters, present participle watering, simple past and past participle watered)\n

    \n\n
    1. (transitive) To pour water into the soil surrounding (plants).\n
      • 1900, L. Frank Baum, chapter 24, in The Wonderful Wizard of Oz:
        Aunt Em had just come out of the house to water the cabbages when she looked up and saw Dorothy running toward her.
      2. \n
    2. (transitive) To wet or supply with water; to moisten; to overflow with water; to irrigate.\n
      • 1667, John Milton, \u201cBook IX\u201d, in Paradise Lost. [\u2026], London: [\u2026] [Samuel Simmons], and are to be sold by Peter Parker [\u2026]; [a]nd by Robert Boulter [\u2026]; [a]nd Matthias Walker, [\u2026], \u2192OCLC; republished as Paradise Lost in Ten Books: [\u2026], London: Basil Montagu Pickering [\u2026], 1873, \u2192OCLC:
        tears watering the ground
        • \n
      • 1847 November 1, Henry Wadsworth Longfellow, Evangeline, a Tale of Acadie, Boston, Mass.: William D. Ticknor & Company, \u2192OCLC, part I:
        Men whose lives glided on like rivers that water the woodlands.
      3. \n
    4. (transitive) To provide (animals) with water for drinking.\n
      I need to water the cattle.
      \n
      • 2008, Oisin Curran, Mopus, page 34:
        While they watered and washed their horses, I talked with a red-capped man, some kind of chief.
      5. \n
    5. (intransitive) To get or take in water.\n
      The ship put into port to water.
      \n
      • 1944 January and February, W. McGowan Gradon, \u201cForres as a Railway Centre\u201d, in Railway Magazine, page 23:
        After working the 1.30 p.m. through train from Forres to Aberdeen as far as Elgin, she returns tender first with a local passenger train and is then coaled and watered at Forres shed, and eventually works back to Perth on the 10.20 p.m. through freight.
      6. \n
    6. (transitive, colloquial) To urinate onto.\n
      Synonym: see Thesaurus:urinate
      \n
      Nature called, so I stepped into the woods and watered a tree.
      7. \n
    7. (transitive) To dilute.\n
      Synonym: water down
      \n
      Antonym: refine
      \n
      Can you water the whisky, please?
      8. \n
    8. (transitive, dated, finance) To overvalue (securities), especially through deceptive accounting.\n
      • 1930 April 10, \u201cCalls Rail Holding Companies Threat\u201d, in The Sun:
        such agencies would make it possible for the railroads to water stock and evade the law subjecting security issues to public regulation
      9. \n
    9. (intransitive) To fill with or secrete water or similar liquid.\n
      Chopping onions makes my eyes water.
      \n
      The smell of fried onions makes my mouth water.
      10. \n
    10. (transitive) To wet and calender, as cloth, so as to impart to it a lustrous appearance in wavy lines; to diversify with wavelike lines.\n
      to water silk

        1. (uncountable) water (an inorganic compound (of molecular formula H2O) found at room temperature and pressure as a clear liquid; it is present naturally as rain, and found in rivers, lakes and seas; its solid form is ice and its gaseous form is steam)\n
