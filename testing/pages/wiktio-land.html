    Noun

    [edit]
    \n

    land (countable and uncountable, plural lands)\n

    \n
    1. The part of Earth which is not covered by oceans or other bodies of water.\n
      Most insects live on land.
      2. \n
    2. Real estate or landed property; a partitioned and measurable area which is owned and acquired and on which buildings and structures can be built and erected.\n
      There are 50 acres of land in this estate.
      3. \n
    3. A country or region.\n
      They come from a faraway land.
      4. \n
    4. A person's country of origin and/or homeplace; homeland.
      5. \n
    5. The soil, in respect to its nature or quality for farming.\n
      wet land
      \n
      good or bad land for growing potatoes
      6. \n
    6. (often in combination) Realm, domain.\n
      I'm going to Disneyland.
      \n
      Maybe that's how it works in TV-land, but not in the real world.
      7. \n
    8. (agriculture) The ground left unploughed between furrows.\n
      Synonym: furlong
      9. \n
    10. (agriculture) Any of several portions into which a field is divided for ploughing.\n
      Synonym: furlong
      \n
      Coordinate terms: headland, furlong
      11. \n
    11. (Ireland, colloquial) A shock or fright.\n
      He got an awful land when the police arrived.
      12. \n
    12. (electronics) A conducting area on a board or chip which can be used for connecting wires.
      13. \n
    13. On a compact disc or similar recording medium, an area of the medium which does not have pits.\n
      • 1935, H. Courtney Bryson, The Gramophone Record, page 72:
        Now, assume that the recording is being done with 100 grooves per inch, and that the record groove is .006 inch wide. This means that the land on either side on any given groove in the absence of sound waves is .004 inch.
      14. \n
    14. (travel) The non-airline portion of an itinerary. Hotel, tours, cruises, etc.\n
      Our city offices sell a lot more land than our suburban offices.
      15. \n
    15. (obsolete) The ground or floor.\n
      • 1596, Edmund Spenser, \u201cBook V, Canto VII\u201d, in The Faerie Queene. [\u2026], London: [\u2026] [John Wolfe] for William Ponsonbie, \u2192OCLC, stanza 7:
        her selfe vppon the land / She did prostrate
      16. \n
    16. (nautical) The lap of the strakes in a clinker-built boat; the lap of plates in an iron vessel; called also landing.[1]
      17. \n
    17. In any surface prepared with indentations, perforations, or grooves, that part of the surface which is not so treated, such as the level part of a millstone between the furrows.\n
      1. (ballistics) The space between the rifling grooves in a gun.\n
        • 2008 August 1, Lisa Steele, \u201cBallistics\u201d, in Eric York Drogin, editor, Science for Lawyers, American Bar Association, page 16:
          The FBI maintains a database, the General Rifling Characteristics (GRC) file, which is organized by caliber, number of lands and grooves, direction of twist, and width of lands and grooves, to help an examiner figure out the origin of a recovered bullet.
          • \n
        • 2012 November 15, \u201cOne Way to Get Off\u201d, in Elementary, season 1, episode 7, spoken by Sherlock Holmes (Jonny Lee Miller):
          The human eye is a precision instrument. It can detect grooves and lands on a slug more efficiently than any computer.
      18. \n
    18. (Scotland, historical) A group of dwellings or tenements under one roof and having a common entry.

    Verb

    [edit]
    \n

    land (third-person singular simple present lands, present participle landing, simple past and past participle landed)\n

    \n
    1. (intransitive) To descend to a surface, especially from the air.\n
      Synonym: alight
      \n
      The plane is about to land.
      2. \n
    2. (dated) To alight, to descend from a vehicle.\n
      • 1859, \u201cRules adopted by the Sixth Avenue Railway, N. Y.\u201d, quoted in Alexander Easton, A Practical Treatise on Street or Horse-Power Railways, page 108:\n
        10. You will be civil and attentive to passengers, giving proper assistance to ladies and children getting in or out, and never start the car before passengers are fairly received or landed.
      3. \n
    3. (intransitive) To come into rest.
      4. \n
    4. (intransitive) To arrive on land, especially a shore or dock, from a body of water.\n
      • 1981, A Pictorial History of the Republic of China: Its Founding and Development‎[1], volume II, Taipei: Modern China Press, \u2192OCLC, page 303, column 1:
        Tatan and Erhtan are two small islands in the sea southwest of Kinmen. [\u2026] A contingent of some 30 Communist troops tried to land at Erhtan, but were disarmed by Government defenders.
      5. \n
    5. (transitive) To bring to land.\n
      It can be tricky to land a helicopter.
      \n
      Use the net to land the fish.
      \n
      • c. 1591\u20131592 (date written), William Shakespeare, \u201cThe Third Part of Henry the Sixt, [\u2026]\u201d, in Mr. William Shakespeares Comedies, Histories, & Tragedies [\u2026] (First Folio), London: [\u2026] Isaac Iaggard, and Ed[ward] Blount, published 1623, \u2192OCLC, [Act III, scene iii]:
        I'll undertake to land them on our coast.
      6. \n
    6. (transitive, informal) To capture or arrest.\n
      • 1920 June, The Electrical Experimenter, New York, page 151, column 3:
        `He told me that he was certain that Coates shot at him. We threw out a drag and landed Coates within an hour.'
      7. \n
    7. (transitive) To acquire; to secure.\n
      She landed a job at the company.
      \n
      • 2012 May 5, Phil McNulty, \u201cChelsea 2-1 Liverpool\u201d, in BBC Sport‎[2]:
        As Di Matteo celebrated and captain John Terry raised the trophy for the fourth time, the Italian increased his claims to become the permanent successor to Andre Villas-Boas by landing a trophy.
        • \n
      • 2024 May 1, \u201cNetwork News: Danes plan fully automated trains\u201d, in RAIL, number 1008, page 18:
        Siemens has landed a contract to upgrade signalling on the entire 170km (105-mile) S-Bane suburban network in Copenhagen to pave the way for fully automated trains.
      8. \n
    8. (slang, transitive) To succeed in having sexual relations with; to score.\n
      Too ugly to ever land a chick.
      9. \n
    9. (transitive, of a blow) To deliver.\n
      If you land a knockout blow, you\u2019ll win the match.
      10. \n
    10. (intransitive, of a punch) To connect (to arrive at an intended target).\n
      If the punches land, you might lose a few teeth!
      \n
      • 2025 August 22, Tom Nichols, \u201cMAGA World Is So Close to Getting It\u201d, in The Atlantic‎[3]:
        Vice President J. D. Vance has lashed out at [Gavin] Newsom, telling Fox that the Californian\u2019s attacks aren\u2019t landing, because his trolling \u201cignores the fundamental genius of President Trump\u2019s political success, which is that he\u2019s authentic\u201d; [\u2026] .
      11. \n
    11. (intransitive, figurative) To go down well with an audience.\n
      Some of the comedian's jokes failed to land.
      \n
      • 2023 January 13, Anonymous (\"Jackal Comment\"), 11:08 from the start, in CORRECTIONS Episode 68: Week of Monday, January 9 (Late Night with Seth Meyers)‎[4], YouTube:
        We told an Amelia Earhart joke yesterday\u2014did not go great with the audience. Someone wrote: \"You can't be surprised when an Amelia Earhart joke doesn't land.\"
        [punning on literal sense 1]

      Usage notes

      [edit]
      \n
      • Using the word land is the most common way to form country names. This can be done in one of two ways:\n
        • Prefixing the name of a people to the word land. Ex: Franca (\u201cFrench person\u201d) \u2192 Francland (\u201cFrance\u201d), Sw\u0113o (\u201cSwede\u201d) \u2192 Sw\u0113oland (\u201cSweden\u201d), and *Unger (\u201ca Hungarian\u201d) \u2192 Ungerland (\u201cHungary\u201d).
          • \n
        • Prefacing land with the genitive plural form of a people, producing the literal meaning \u201cland of ____ people.\u201d Ex: Egypta land (\u201cEgypt\u201d, literally \u201cland of the Egyptians\u201d), Si\u0121elhearwena land (\u201cEthiopia\u201d, literally \u201cland of the Ethiopians\u201d).
        • \n
      • However, country names can also be formed other ways. For instance, words other than land are used: Dene (\u201ca Dane\u201d) \u2192 Denemearc (\u201cDenmark\u201d, literally \u201cDane borderland\u201d). It is also very common to use the name of a people for the country they inhabit: On \u00fe\u0101m dagum w\u00e6s Alexander \u0121eboren on Cr\u0113cum sw\u0101 sw\u0101 mi\u010bel \u0233st c\u014dme ofer ealne middan\u0121eard (\u201cIn those days, Alexander was born in Greece [lit. in the Greeks] like a great storm coming over the whole world\u201d), Ymb tw\u0101 \u0121\u0113ar \u00fe\u00e6s \u00fee h\u0113 c\u014dm of Francum, h\u0113 \u0121ef\u014dr (\u201dTwo years after he came from France [lit. from the Franks], he died\u201d). In addition, country names are sometimes loaned directly from Latin: Arabia, Isr\u0101h\u0113l, Italia, Syria. Finally, some country names are simply idiomatic: Nor\u00fewe\u0121 (\u201cNorway\u201d, literally \u201cnorth way\u201d).
        • \n
      • Unlike most words, land undergoes i-umlaut when combined with the suffix -is\u010b: inlendis\u010b (\u201cnative\u201d), uplendis\u010b (\u201crural\u201d).
