Etymology

[edit]
\n

From Middle High German h\u0113rre, h\u00ebrre, from Old High German h\u0113riro, h\u0113rro, originally comparative of h\u0113r (\u201cgrey-haired, noble, venerable\u201d, whence German hehr), a semantic loan from Latin senior (\u201celder\u201d). Ultimately from Proto-Germanic *hairaz (\u201cgrey\u201d).\n

Cognate with Dutch heer, Old English h\u0101r, English hoar, Old Norse h\u00e1rr; compare also modern German hehr (\u201cnoble, holy\u201d), herrschen (\u201cto rule\u201d).\n

Usage notes

[edit]
\n
  • Omitting Herr (or the female form Frau) when addressing a person with their last name is usually perceived as disrespectful, but it is more common when speaking about someone who is not present, except in formal contexts. However, there may be contextual pitfalls and regional differences, which makes it advisable for learners not to leave out Herr (and Frau).
    • \n
  • When people address each other with their last name, but say du to each other, the words Herr and Frau are always left out in most regions. In parts of western Germany, however, there is (or was) a system of saying du and Herr/Frau among coworkers.
    • \n
  • There are three different female equivalents of Herr, each covering different senses: Herrin, Dame, and Frau.
\n

Declension

[edit]
\n
  • The forms Herrn and Herren were originally simple phonetic/graphic variants. Both were used for the singular and plural inflections. In contemporary standard German they are\u2014usually\u2014distinguished functionally, Herrn being the inflected singular, Herren the plural.

Etymology

[edit]
\n

From Middle High German h\u0113rre, h\u00ebrre, from Old High German h\u0113riro, h\u0113rro (\u201cgrey, grey-haired\u201d), comparative of h\u0113r (\u201cnoble, venerable\u201d, whence German hehr), a semantic loan from Latin senior (\u201celder\u201d). Ultimately from Proto-West Germanic *hair, from Proto-Germanic *hairaz (\u201cgrey\u201d). Cognate with Dutch heer, Old English h\u0101r, English hoar, Old Norse h\u00e1rr.\n
