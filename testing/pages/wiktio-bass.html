    Noun

    [edit]
    \n

    bass (plural basses)\n

    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    \n
    A bass voice singing a soprano part
    \n
    \n\n
    1. A low spectrum of sound tones.\n
      Peter adjusted the equalizer on his audio equipment to emphasize the bass.
      2. \n
    2. (music) A section of a musical group that produces low-pitched sound, lower than the baritone and tenor.\n
      The conductor preferred to situate the bass in the middle rear, rather than to one side of the orchestra.
      3. \n
    3. (music) One who sings in the bass range.\n
      Halfway through middle school, Edgar morphed from a soprano to a bass, much to the amazement and amusement of his fellow choristers.
      4. \n
    4. (music) An instrument that plays in the bass range, in particular a double bass, bass guitar, electric bass or bass synthesiser.\n
      The musician swung the bass over his head like an axe and smashed it into the amplifier, creating a discordant howl of noise.
      5. \n
    5. (music notation) The clef sign that indicates that the pitch of the notes is below middle C; a bass clef.\n
      The score had been written without the treble and bass, but it was easy to pick out which was which based on the location of the notes on the staff.

    Etymology 2

    [edit]
    \n
      \n\n

      From Middle English bace, bas, alteration of bars, from Old English b\u00e6rs (\u201ca fish, perch\u201d), from Proto-West Germanic *bars, from Proto-Germanic *barsaz (\u201cperch\u201d, literally \u201cprickly\u201d), from Proto-Indo-European *b\u02b0\u00f3rsos (\u201cprickle, thorn, scale\u201d). Cognate with Dutch baars (\u201cperch, bass\u201d), German Barsch (\u201cperch\u201d). More at barse.\n

        German

        [edit]
        \n

        Etymology

        [edit]
        \n

        From Middle High German baz, from Old High German baz, from Proto-Germanic *batiz (\u201cbetter\u201d, adverb), comparative of *wela (\u201cwell\u201d), whence German wohl. Represents the old adverb form of besser, whose adverbial use developed in Middle High German. Attributive use of bass, in turn, is much more recent and has remained rare.\n

        \n

        Adverb

        [edit]
        \n

        bass\n

        \n
        1. (dated higher register, sometimes humorous) greatly, totally, immensely; used with erstaunt (\u201castonished\u201d) or, less often, other words expressing a sudden reaction/emotion\n
          Ich war bass erstaunt. \u2015 I was totally astonished.
          \n
          Das hat mich bass \u00fcberrascht. \u2015 That greatly surprised me.
          2. \n
        2. (obsolete) better, more properly, readily
