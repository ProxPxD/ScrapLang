Etymology 1

[edit]
\n

See board.\n

Noun

[edit]
\n

bord (plural bords)\n

\n
  1. Obsolete form of board. [11th\u201317th c.]\n
    • 1898, Herbert William Hughes, A Text-book of Coal-mining, page 154:
      A jenkin is then carried up the pillar alongside the old bords [\u2026]
    2. \n
  2. Obsolete form of bourd. [14th\u201317th c.]

[edit]

Derived terms
[edit]

Etymology 2

[edit]
\n

From board, which is also a less common variant of bord; probably from the former practice of laying boards in mine passageways to form a relatively smooth surface along which the coal was dragged in sledges.[1]\n

\n

Noun

[edit]
\n

bord (plural bords)\n

\n
  1. (mining) The coalface parallel to the natural fissures.

Noun

[edit]
\n

bord (plural bordes or borden)\n

\n
  1. A board or slab (usually of wood)
    2. \n
  2. A piece of wood for writing upon.
    3. \n
  3. A table (especially one used for craftsmanship).\n
    1. (religion) An altar; a table used for religious purposes.
      2. \n
    2. A dining table or its surface.
    4. \n
  4. A serving or helping of food and drink; nourishment.
    5. \n
  5. A seafaring vessel; a boat.
    6. \n
  6. The direction a boat is headed in.
    7. \n
  7. A shield (board of protective armour).
